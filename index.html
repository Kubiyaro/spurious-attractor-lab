A Hopfield network is an associative memory — trained on patterns, it can recover them from noise. John Hopfield showed in 1982 that the theoretical storage capacity is ~0.14 × N neurons. At 64 neurons per character, that's roughly 9 stable memories.

Pattern Retrieval stores all 94 printable ASCII characters — a 10.4× overload. When you force this much information into a network that small, something unexpected happens. The energy landscape buckles. New stable minima appear: attractors that correspond to no training pattern. Mixtures. Hybrids. Hallucinations.

These are spurious states — the network's false memories. Feed it pure noise and instead of recalling any character, it falls into one of these phantom attractors. A new glyph. A pattern that was never stored, yet the network treats as just as real as any other. Hopfield called this phenomenon spurious memories. We call it ghost art.
